The True Story of the Internet: Browser Wars

After the public gained access to the internet it was difficult to navigate. A new piece of software emerged to alleviate that headache.

Once the internet was created and many sites were available to access; there was not a very intuitive way to access that information, especially in a point and click format similar to how we do it today in 2020. Thus came the inception of a software company called NetScape (originally Mosaic). They were the company who developed the first web browser anyone could use it and was much simpler than previous ways of access in the internet. In 1993 Marc Andreessen a graduate student at University of Illinois at Champaign-Urbana along with 6 peers made a graphical user interface to easily browse websites. This attracted the attention of Jim Clarke, due to the graphical user interface becoming insanely popular over the course of the next 2 years.

Urbana Campus

In April 1994 Jim Clarke, an investor and founder of Silicon Graphics, contacted Marc Andreesen and they formed a company which would be known as Netscape Communications. Bill Gates the co-founder of Microsoft, did not believe that the world wide web would be a technology that would stick around. He thought that the WWW should be heavily monitored and only accessible with pay walls and not for everyone for free. Microsoft Network was made which sort of web portal for people to access the WWW. He wanted to have monopoly of consumer PCs.

Jim Clarke

Netscape Communications main goal was to “take over the world” with their new software which allowed the WWW in the hands of any regular person in an easy manner. They started working on the next version of Mosaic called Netscape Navigator in October 1994. They were a budding startup and grew exponentially fast. The internet age had begun, they amassed a million downloads very quickly. Microsoft grew wary of Netscape because it felt as if its iron grip on the computing industry had been loosened. Where computers could not need the Windows Operating System and use only a browser to do all the work on a computer.

A critical rule that ensures the blooming of computer technology and the internet is Moore Rule. Moore's Law refers to Moore's perception that the number of transistors on a microchip doubles every two years, though the cost of computers is halved. Moore's Law states that we can expect the speed and capability of our computers to increase every couple of years, and we will pay less for them. Another tenet of Moore's Law asserts that this growth is exponential, which means it would grow faster and faster.

Figure1: the Moore Rule

More and more companies are established, the dot com bubble is becoming bigger and bigger. The dot com bubble was a rapid rise in U.S. technology stock equity valuations fueled by investments in internet-based companies during the bull market in the late 1990s. During the dotcom bubble, the value of equity markets grew exponentially, with the technology-dominated Nasdaq index rising from under 1,000 to more than 5,000 between the years 1995 and 2000. In 2001 and through 2002 the bubble burst, with equities entering a bear market.

Figure1: Bubble

The crash that followed saw the Nasdaq index, which had risen five-fold between 1995 and 2000, tumble from a peak of 5,048.62 on March 10, 2000, to 1,139.90 on Oct 4, 2002, a 76.81% fall. By the end of 2001, most dotcom stocks had gone bust. Even the share prices of blue-chip technology stocks like Cisco, Intel and Oracle lost more than 80% of their value. It would take 15 years for the Nasdaq to regain its dotcom peak, which it did on April 23, 2015.

The bubble ultimately burst in a spectacular fashion, leaving many investors facing steep losses and several internet companies going bust. Companies that famously survived the bubble include Amazon, eBay. Finally, people get to realize the true aim of the internet: create values, and lead the further development of the internet.
